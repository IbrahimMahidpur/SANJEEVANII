# Cognitive Distortions in CBT

## All-or-Nothing Thinking (Black-and-White Thinking)
Viewing situations in only two categories instead of on a continuum.
Example: "If I'm not perfect, I'm a total failure."
Challenge: Look for shades of gray. Most situations aren't completely good or bad.

## Catastrophizing
Expecting the worst possible outcome.
Example: "If I fail this test, my life is ruined."
Challenge: What's the realistic worst case? What's the most likely outcome?

## Overgeneralization
Making broad interpretations from a single or few events.
Example: "I didn't get the job. I'll never find employment."
Challenge: Is this always true? What are the exceptions?

## Mind Reading
Assuming you know what others are thinking without evidence.
Example: "They think I'm incompetent."
Challenge: What's the evidence? Could there be other explanations?

## Fortune Telling
Predicting negative outcomes without evidence.
Example: "I know the presentation will go badly."
Challenge: What evidence supports this? What if you're wrong?

## Emotional Reasoning
Believing that feelings reflect reality.
Example: "I feel anxious, so something bad must happen."
Challenge: Feelings aren't facts. What's the objective evidence?

## Should Statements
Using "should," "must," or "ought" creates unrealistic expectations.
Example: "I should always be productive."
Challenge: Replace with preferences: "I'd prefer to be productive, but it's okay if I'm not always."

## Labeling
Assigning global negative traits based on specific behaviors.
Example: "I made a mistake, so I'm an idiot."
Challenge: Separate behavior from identity. Everyone makes mistakes.

## Personalization
Blaming yourself for things outside your control.
Example: "My friend is upset, it must be my fault."
Challenge: What other factors might be involved?

## Discounting the Positive
Rejecting positive experiences as not counting.
Example: "They only complimented me to be nice."
Challenge: Accept positive feedback at face value.
